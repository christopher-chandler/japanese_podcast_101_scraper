Lesson Focus

Key Expression for this Lesson: to Report a Lost Bag at a Station Office

"Excuse me, I left my bag on the train."

(sumimasen) is an expression that is used when you start a conversation with an unfamiliar person or when you try to request something. (Densha ni kaban o wasureta) means "I left my bag on the train." There is (no desu ga) at the end of sentence. Adding (no desu ga) at the end of sentence can soften the expression by using it as the reason for needing help, and also it makes the sentence more polite. In this sentence, (Densha ni kaban o wasureta) is the situation. "The situation (no desu ga)" implies that you need help or you want to request something. So then, people can imagine that you want to say (kaban o sagashite kudasai) which means "Can you find my bag?"

Key Expression for this Lesson: Describing Your Lost Bag

"It's a black nylon bag."

When you describe your lost property like a bag, you can explain it in this word order. "(kuroi) color" "(nairon) material" (no) "(kaban) type."

Tip: Variations of Colors

Some colors have adjectives, but some don't. For example, - "black," - "white," - "red," - "blue," - "yellow" have adjectives like (kuroi) (shiroi) (akai) (aoi) (kiiroi). But - "green," - "orange," - "gray," - "pink" don't have adjectives. So we add (no) after these colors and they can be modifiers, which are used like adjectives.

For example:



  1. "black, white, red, blue, yellow"


  2. "green, orange, gray, pink"

Key Vocabulary & Phrases

, ichi-ry-me, "the first car"

(ry) is a counter to count train cars, and (me) is a suffix for making ordinal numbers. You just say the number first and add (ry-me) when pointing out the train car number.

For examples:



"the first car"



"the fourth car"



"the seventh car"

 

()/... , kurai/... no h, "somewhere in a certain direction"

() (kurai, also pronounced gurai) is an adverbial particle used to roughly indicate amount or extent. The pronunciation changes depending on the word before (kurai). If you don't remember the exact car you were on but you think you were on somewhere in the middle, you can say (mannaka gurai).

... (... no h) means "direction" or "somewhere in a certain direction." So this is used with directions like (mae), (ushiro), (acchi) and so on.

For example:



"somewhere in the middle"



"somewhere in the front"



"somewhere in the back"



"somewhere in that area"

Cultural Insights

There's a Good Chance You'll Get Your Lost Property Returned in Japan

 

Reporting to the lost and found office is pretty effective in Japan. You can get things back if you lost them because many Japanese people bring lost property to the nearest police station or lost and found office when they come across it. Most stations keep lost property for a while, like one week or one month, and then they will send it to the lost and found office if no one comes to get them. So first you should ask the nearest station staff and tell them the information about your lost property.
